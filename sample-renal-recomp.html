The Plan · Week by Week

24 weeks, around your dialysis week.

Your week is fixed around dialysis on Tuesday, Thursday, and Saturday. Resistance training lands on the non-dialysis days — Monday, Wednesday, Friday — when you’re euvolemic and electrolytes have re-equilibrated. Dialysis days are rest and recovery. Sunday stays deliberately light because you’re anuric and the long potassium gap runs until Tuesday. Loads progress every ~4 weeks. Tap a week, then any day.

Nutrition · Built for your kidneys

Eating for muscle without overloading your labs.

Three constraints shape every choice: enough protein to build muscle, low enough phosphorus and potassium to stay safe, and fluid within your limit. The reasoning is below — your renal dietitian and nephrology team set your exact numbers, and this is built to support that, not replace it.

Body comp

Recomposition, with a floor

Build lean mass and trim visceral fat — but there’s a hard floor: in dialysis, very low body fat is linked to higher mortality (the “obesity paradox”). Don’t drive body fat below ~15%. Waist circumference (under ~90 cm) is a better target than the scale, and calories stay around 2,300 (~25 kcal/kg) — going lower triggers muscle breakdown.

Protein vs phosphate

High-quality, low-phosphate protein

Aim for the protein target your renal dietitian sets (around 1.0–1.2 g/kg). Choose low-PPR sources — phosphorus per gram of protein. Egg whites are unbeatable (PPR ~1–2); unprocessed chicken, beef, and fish sit at ~8–12; a specially formulated low-phosphorus whey isolate works too. Boiling or pressure-cooking meat and discarding the water cuts phosphorus by roughly half.

Potassium & fluid

Anuric — so K⁺ and fluid stay tight

You clear nothing between sessions, so potassium and fluid both have to stay controlled. Favor lower-potassium foods, leach high-K⁺ vegetables, and keep fluid inside your daily allowance. Your AM weight is the fluid gauge — a steady number means steady IDWG.

Processed and phosphate-additive foods, plus dairy-heavy proteins — additive phosphorus is nearly 100% absorbed.

Cooking trick: boiling or pressure-cooking sliced meat and discarding the water removes about half the phosphorus while keeping the protein.

Renal-smart swaps

  • Protein anchor → egg whites, fresh fish / chicken
  • Whole-grain (high P/K) → white rice / white bread
  • Potatoes → leached / double-boiled, smaller portion
  • Processed meats & namkeen → fresh, unprocessed
  • Cook with → olive oil (calorie-dense, P/K-neutral)
  • Binders → Renvela with each protein meal, as prescribed

Why white carbs here

  • This is the opposite of generic adviceFor most people, whole grains win. On a renal diet, refined white rice and bread are often the safer pick — less phosphorus and potassium — and you need the calories without the mineral load.
  • Calories protect muscleClean fats and white carbs hit the ~2,300 kcal floor without raising phosphate or potassium. Under-eating, not over-eating, is the bigger threat to your lean mass.
Quick reference · around dialysis

Eating & meds around your sessions

  • Protein, evenly spreadDialysis blunts the muscle-building response to a meal (anabolic resistance), and a session removes ~10–17 g of amino acids. Spreading protein across the day — and pairing it with training — beats one big serving.
  • Protein during the runIf your unit allows it, egg whites or a low-phosphorus whey during the first part of dialysis offsets those amino-acid losses. Confirm with your team before doing this.
  • Binders, as prescribedRenvela with each phosphorus-containing meal; tenapanor before meals as its label specifies (typically the first and last meal of the day), and never right before a dialysis session. This plan reinforces your existing prescription; it does not set or change doses. That’s your nephrologist and pharmacist.
  • Fluid & your AM weightSpread your fluid allowance through the day and weigh each morning. Large interdialytic weight gain means harder fluid removal and more post-dialysis fatigue.

Training safely on dialysis

The rules that keep this safe.

Resistance training is shown to be safe and effective in hemodialysis — within boundaries. These are the boundaries. Read them with your nephrology team and treat them as non-negotiable.

  • Get cleared firstBefore progressive loading: a baseline DXA, PTH confirmed within the KDIGO target (2–9× the upper limit of normal), and calcium / phosphate / alkaline phosphatase well-controlled. Cardiac screening per your team’s judgment.
  • The load ceiling3 sets × 8–12 reps at RPE 7–8 (~70–80% 1RM), with controlled lifting and lowering. No maximal singles, no explosive or ballistic lifting — fracture and tendon-rupture risk is elevated in CKD-MBD, and true heavy strength work has not been studied in dialysis.
  • Protect the fistula armNo weight-bearing, heavy gripping, blood-pressure cuffs, or tourniquets on the access arm — ever. Use chest-supported and cable movements so you can train the back without gripping hard. Your vascular surgeon’s rules override these.
  • Timing around dialysisTrain on non-dialysis days. Avoid heavy lifting right after a session — hypovolemia, low potassium, arrhythmia risk, and deep fatigue stack up post-run. Wednesday and Friday fall the day after dialysis, when you’re freshly cleared — the safest windows. Monday falls at the end of the long weekend gap, when fluid and potassium are highest, so confirm with your team whether to keep, lighten, or move that session. The best day relative to dialysis is individual and isn’t fully settled in the evidence.
  • The anuric weekend gapKeep Sunday genuinely light. Muscle breakdown releases potassium you can’t clear until Tuesday’s session, so heavy work into that gap is a real arrhythmia risk.
  • Stop signs — hold and call your teamPre-dialysis K⁺ over 6.0 mEq/L · BP over 180 or under 90 mmHg · new joint or bone pain or fracture symptoms · severe post-dialysis fatigue lasting beyond ~8 hours.
Workouts · Tap any exercise

The training, the safe way.

Lower, Push, and Pull sessions on your non-dialysis days. Every set sits in the 8–12 rep range at RPE 7–8 — hard but never maximal — with controlled, dynamic movements that are bone-protective. Tap any exercise for sets, a tempo animation, form cues, and a demo. Pulls are chest-supported or cable-based to keep load off your fistula arm.
